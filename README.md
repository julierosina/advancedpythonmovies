# Investigating predictors of movie quality and visiblity
This paper investigates whether the structural features of a film predict audience quality and platform visibility in the same way, or whether the two outcomes are driven by different logics. Drawing on the TMDB 5000 Movie Dataset, we train logistic regression and decision tree classifiers on 4,388 films to predict two binary outcomes: high quality (top quartile of audience ratings) and high visibility (top quartile of popularity score). Our results confirm that the two dimensions diverge substantially. Quality is best predicted by runtime, release year, and content-related genre signals such as documentary and drama, and is better captured by a linear model. Visibility is best predicted by budget, recency, and blockbuster genre indicators such as action and adventure, and is better captured by a nonlinear decision tree that concentrates almost all predictive power in four features. These findings empirically reproduce, on a large contemporary dataset, the two-path model of cinematic success proposed by Holbrook & Addis (2008), and suggest that platform amplification mechanisms reinforce structural inequalities in cultural exposure that are independent of film quality.
